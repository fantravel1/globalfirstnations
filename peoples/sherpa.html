Who Are the Sherpa?

The Sherpa are an ethnic group native to the Himalayan regions of Nepal, primarily the Khumbu region below Mount Everest. Renowned worldwide for their mountaineering skills and high-altitude expertise, the Sherpa possess unique physiological adaptations to extreme altitude and maintain rich Tibetan Buddhist traditions.

Culture & Traditions

Tibetan Buddhism

Sherpa practice Tibetan Buddhism with monasteries like Tengboche serving as spiritual centers. Prayer wheels, mani stones, and prayer flags are ubiquitous. The Mani Rimdu festival features masked dances and Buddhist teachings celebrating their deep spiritual heritage.

Mountaineering Excellence

Sherpa mountaineering expertise is legendary. They carry heavy loads, fix ropes, establish camps, and rescue climbers at extreme altitudes. Many Sherpa have summited Everest dozens of times. Tenzing Norgay's 1953 summit with Edmund Hillary brought global recognition to Sherpa mountaineering prowess.

Traditional Livelihood

Beyond mountaineering, Sherpa practice high-altitude agriculture and yak herding. They grow potatoes, barley, and buckwheat in terraced fields. Yaks provide meat, milk, wool, and transportation in this challenging mountain environment.
