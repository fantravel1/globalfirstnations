The Largest Indigenous Nation

The Navajo (who call themselves Din√©, meaning "The People") are the largest federally recognized Native American nation in the United States, with a reservation spanning over 27,000 square miles across Arizona, New Mexico, Utah, and Colorado. The Navajo Nation is larger than ten U.S. states and maintains significant political sovereignty. Known for their rich artistic traditions‚Äîparticularly weaving and silversmithing‚Äîas well as their crucial role in World War II as Code Talkers, the Navajo have maintained strong cultural identity while adapting to contemporary American society.

Navajo culture is deeply rooted in harmony with nature and the universe, expressed through the concept of h√≥zh«´ÃÅ (beauty, harmony, balance, and wellness). This philosophy guides all aspects of Din√© life, from daily activities to ceremonial practices. Traditional Navajo society is matrilineal, with clan membership passed through the mother's line. The Navajo recognize over 60 clans, and clan relationships govern social interactions and marriage rules.

The Navajo Nation operates with an elected government including a president, vice president, and 24-member council, governing one of the most sophisticated tribal governments in North America. From their ancestral homeland bounded by four sacred mountains, the Din√© continue to practice traditional ceremonies, create world-renowned art, and assert sovereignty while navigating the challenges of the 21st century.

Code Talkers Legacy: During World War II, Navajo Code Talkers created an unbreakable military code based on the Navajo language. The code was never cracked by Axis powers and is credited with saving countless American lives, particularly in the Pacific theater. This remarkable contribution to American military history stands as a proud moment in Navajo history.
