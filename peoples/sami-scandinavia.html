Reindeer Herding

Reindeer herding defines Sami identity even though only about 10% of Sami actively herd today. Sami reindeer are semi-domesticated, ranging freely across vast territories while herders follow and manage them through seasonal migrations between coastal and inland pastures. Each reindeer is individually owned, identified by distinctive ear notches. Herding rights are legally protected in Norway and Sweden, restricted to Sami families with herding traditions. Modern herders use snowmobiles and helicopters alongside traditional methods, adapting ancient practices to contemporary conditions.

Joik: The Indigenous Music

The joik is a unique Sami vocal tradition‚Äînot a song about something but a song that is something. A joik captures the essence of a person, animal, or place rather than describing it. Each person may have their own joik, composed spontaneously at birth or another significant moment. Joiking was suppressed by Christian missionaries who considered it shamanic and sinful. The tradition survived underground and has experienced revival, with contemporary artists fusing traditional joik with modern genres. UNESCO recognizes joik as intangible cultural heritage.

Young Sami increasingly wear g√°kti for graduations, weddings, and cultural events, asserting identity in contemporary contexts.

Rights Struggles and Victories

The Sami faced centuries of assimilation policies‚Äîchildren sent to boarding schools, language suppressed, traditional practices discouraged. Since the 1970s, Sami political activism has achieved significant gains: Sami parliaments in Norway, Sweden, and Finland advise governments on Sami affairs; languages receive official protection; and land and resource rights are increasingly recognized. The 2020 Swedish case Girjas confirmed Sami exclusive hunting and fishing rights on traditional lands. Climate change now threatens reindeer herding as warming alters vegetation and ice conditions, creating new challenges for Arctic indigenous life.
