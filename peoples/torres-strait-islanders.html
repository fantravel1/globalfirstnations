Who Are the Torres Strait Islanders?

Torres Strait Islanders are the Indigenous peoples of the Torres Strait Islandsâ€”over 270 islands between Australia's Cape York Peninsula and Papua New Guinea. Approximately 70,000 Torres Strait Islanders live in Australia, though most now reside on the mainland rather than the islands. Culturally and linguistically distinct from Aboriginal Australians, Torres Strait Islanders are Melanesian with connections to Papua New Guinea. Recognized as one of Australia's two Indigenous peoples, they maintain distinctive traditions including seafaring, elaborate headdresses (dhari), and the Tombstone Opening ceremony.

Ailan Kastom: Island Custom

Ailan Kastom (Island Custom) governs Torres Strait Islander life, encompassing law, ceremony, kinship, and land/sea management. Each island has specific customs, totems, and sacred sites. The eight-pointed star on the Torres Strait Islander flag represents the island groups. Traditional governance involved councils and elders making decisions affecting their islands. Kastom includes protocols for fishing, sharing resources, and maintaining relationships between islands. Though Christianity (particularly Anglican and Catholic) became dominant, Kastom persists alongside and intertwined with Christian practice.

Maritime Culture

Torres Strait Islanders are quintessential seafarers. Traditional outrigger canoes enabled travel between islands and to Papua New Guinea for trade. Diving for pearl shell, trochus, and beche-de-mer became economically important during colonial times. The sea provides foodâ€”dugong, turtle, fish, and shellfish remain culturally significant. Climate change threatens low-lying islands with inundation; Torres Strait Islanders have become frontline advocates for climate action, filing legal cases arguing that climate inaction violates their human rights.

The Mabo Decision

Eddie Koiki Mabo, from Mer (Murray Island), launched the legal case that transformed Australian law. In 1992, the Mabo decision recognized native titleâ€”overturning the fiction of terra nullius (that Australia was unoccupied before British arrival). This landmark ruling acknowledged that Indigenous land rights could survive colonization. Mabo died before the decision but became an Australian hero. The case emerged from Torres Strait Islander connection to specific islands, demonstrating how cultural attachment to land could translate into legal rights.

Dance and Ceremony

Torres Strait Islander dance is dynamic and distinctive, featuring elaborate costumes, headdresses (dhari) of feathers and shells, and movements often depicting sea creatures and ancestral stories. Dance groups perform at cultural festivals and competitions throughout Australia. The Tombstone Opening ceremony, held years after burial, formally farewells the deceased with feasting, dancing, and grave decoration. Performances combine ancient traditions with contemporary creativity, and Torres Strait Islander artists have contributed significantly to Australian performing arts.
