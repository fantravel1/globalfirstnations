Who Are the Seto?

The Seto (also spelled Setu) are a Finno-Ugric people inhabiting Setomaa, a region straddling the border between southeastern Estonia and Russia's Pskov Oblast. Numbering approximately 13,000, they speak Seto, a South Estonian dialect with distinct vocabulary and grammar, and practice Eastern Orthodox Christianity—distinguishing them from predominantly Lutheran Estonians. Their culture preserves ancient Finnic traditions, including a unique polyphonic singing tradition recognized by UNESCO.

Orthodox Faith in a Lutheran Land

The Seto converted to Eastern Orthodoxy under Russian influence beginning in the 10th-11th centuries, while neighboring Estonians became Lutheran during the Reformation. This religious difference created a cultural boundary that has persisted for centuries. The Seto remained within the Russian Orthodox sphere, building onion-domed churches, venerating icons, and observing Orthodox holidays and fasts.

Yet Seto Orthodoxy blends with older Finnic traditions in ways that would seem heterodox elsewhere. Pre-Christian beliefs in forest spirits, household guardians, and ancestor veneration coexist with Orthodox practice. Sacred springs are blessed by priests but carry older meanings. This synthesis created a distinctive Seto religious culture that is Orthodox in form but Finnic in many underlying concepts.

Leelo: Polyphonic Singing

The Seto are famous for leelo, a unique polyphonic singing tradition recognized by UNESCO as Intangible Cultural Heritage. In leelo, a lead singer (sõnoline) improvises verses while a choir (torrõ) responds with a distinctive drone. The resulting sound—haunting, otherworldly harmonies—is unlike any other European folk music. Leelo accompanies celebrations, work, and ritual, with songs for every occasion from weddings to funerals.

Leelo is traditionally women's music, with female song leaders (lauluimä, "song mothers") composing and leading performances. Songs address love, nature, daily life, and history, with texts often combining traditional formulas with improvised verses. The tradition survived Soviet suppression through private performance and has experienced revival since Estonian independence, with song festivals and teaching programs ensuring transmission to new generations.

Divided Homeland

The 1944 Soviet occupation and subsequent boundary changes divided Setomaa between the Estonian SSR and Russian SFSR. Families were separated by an internal Soviet border that became an international frontier after 1991. Today, approximately two-thirds of historic Setomaa lies in Russia's Pskov Oblast, while one-third is in Estonia's Võru and Põlva counties. Crossing requires passports and visas, fragmenting what was once a unified community.

The Seto in Russia face particular challenges: small, declining population; limited cultural institutions; and assimilation pressure into Russian-speaking society. Estonian Seto maintain more robust cultural infrastructure, with museums, schools teaching Seto language, and official recognition as a distinct indigenous group within Estonia. Cross-border cooperation is limited by geopolitical tensions, particularly since Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine.

Material Culture

Seto material culture includes distinctive silver jewelry—massive chains, brooches, and breast ornaments (sõlg) that indicate wealth and status. Women's traditional dress features layers of elaborately decorated garments, white linen headdresses, and the characteristic silver adornments. These costumes, weighing several kilograms, are worn for festivals, weddings, and cultural performances. Contemporary artisans continue traditional metalwork and textile production.

Seto folk architecture featured log houses with distinctive decorative elements, smoke saunas, and farm buildings adapted to the mixed forest environment. The Seto Farm Museum preserves traditional buildings and lifeways. Food traditions include distinctive breads, dairy products, and dishes reflecting both Estonian and Russian influences, prepared according to Orthodox fasting rules that structure the culinary calendar.

Cultural Revival

Since Estonian independence in 1991, the Seto have experienced significant cultural revival. The Seto language gained official recognition as a regional language. The Seto Congress, an elected representative body, advocates for Seto interests and organizes cultural activities. The annual Seto Kingdom Day playfully crowns a "king" and celebrates Seto identity through music, crafts, and traditional foods.

Young Seto increasingly embrace their heritage, learning language and songs, wearing traditional dress for special occasions, and asserting identity in multiethnic Estonia. Yet challenges remain: the Russian Seto community continues to decline; urbanization draws young people away from traditional villages; and the language requires ongoing effort to transmit. The Seto story illustrates both the vulnerability of small minorities on political borderlands and the resilience of identity when communities organize for cultural survival.
