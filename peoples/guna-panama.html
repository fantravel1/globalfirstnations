Who Are the Guna?

Numbering approximately 80,000, they are renowned for their mola textile art, successful struggle for autonomy, and innovative approaches to tourism and climate adaptation. The Guna maintain one of Latin America's strongest indigenous self-governance systems, controlling their territory with minimal outside interference.

Mola: Wearable Art

The mola is a colorful textile panel created using reverse appliquÃ©â€”cutting through multiple fabric layers to reveal colors beneath. Originally the front and back panels of women's blouses, molas feature geometric patterns, animals, plants, and increasingly modern imagery including helicopters and cartoon characters. Creating a fine mola requires weeks of intricate work. Molas have become internationally collected art objects, providing significant income for Guna women artists. Each mola is unique, expressing the creator's skill and imagination.

The 1925 Revolution

In 1925, the Guna launched a successful armed uprising against Panamanian police who were attempting to suppress indigenous culture, particularly traditional dress and ceremonies. The "Guna Revolution" resulted in Panamanian recognition of Guna autonomy, creating the Comarca (autonomous region) of Guna Yala. This early success in securing indigenous rights preceded similar movements elsewhere by decades. The revolution is commemorated annually, and Guna political organizationâ€”based on community assemblies and elected leadersâ€”remains robust.

Island Living and Climate Change

Most Guna live on small, densely populated coral islands just above sea level. These islands face existential threat from climate change: rising seas, stronger storms, and coral erosion endanger communities. Several villages have already relocated from islands to the mainland. The Guna face difficult decisions about abandoning ancestral islands for higher ground. International attention on Guna climate vulnerability has made them symbols of frontline climate communities, while Guna leaders advocate for global action on emissions.

Controlled Tourism

The Guna regulate tourism in their territory more strictly than most indigenous communities. Visitors must pay fees, follow rules, and often stay in Guna-run accommodations. Photography may require payment. This controlled approach maximizes community benefit while limiting disruptive impacts. Cruise ships are banned from some areas. The Guna demonstrate that indigenous communities can engage with tourism on their own terms, extracting economic benefit while protecting cultural integrity. Their model influences indigenous tourism strategies elsewhere.
