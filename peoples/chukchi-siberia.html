Who Are the Chukchi?

The Chukchi are an indigenous people of the Chukotka Peninsula in Russia's Far East, the closest point between Asia and North America. Divided into maritime hunters who pursue whales and walrus from skin boats and inland reindeer herders, the Chukchi inhabit one of the world's most extreme environments. They successfully resisted Russian colonization for over 150 years, earning a reputation as fierce warriors before eventually being incorporated into the Russian Empire.

Maritime Hunters: Masters of the Ice

Coastal Chukchi are among the world's most skilled marine mammal hunters. Using skin-covered boats (baidara), crews hunt bowhead whales, gray whales, walruses, and seals. Whale hunting is communal‚Äîthe entire village participates in processing a catch that can feed the community for months. Traditional hunting required approaching whales in kayaks to thrust harpoons, demonstrating extraordinary courage and skill. Today, limited traditional hunting continues under international agreements recognizing indigenous subsistence rights.

Yaranga: The Arctic Dwelling

The yaranga is the traditional Chukchi home, a dome-shaped structure framed with whale bones or wooden poles and covered with walrus or reindeer hides. Inside, a smaller sleeping chamber (polog) made of fur provides warmth, while oil lamps heat and light the space. The yaranga's design allows it to withstand hurricane-force Arctic winds. Maritime families traditionally maintained permanent villages while reindeer herders moved their yarangas following their herds across the tundra.

Spiritual World and Shamanism

Traditional Chukchi spirituality centers on animism‚Äîthe belief that all things possess spirits requiring proper respect and relationship. Shamans (enenen) mediate between human and spirit worlds, conducting healing ceremonies, predicting weather, and ensuring hunting success through drumming and trance. The Chukchi cosmos includes multiple worlds connected by the Pole Star. Whale spirits are especially powerful, and successful hunts require proper rituals to thank the whale for offering itself and ensure future abundance.

Cultural Resilience

Soviet-era collectivization devastated traditional Chukchi life, forcing settlement in villages, confiscating reindeer herds, and suppressing shamanic practices. The population declined dramatically. Since 1991, cultural revival efforts have restored some traditional practices. The Chukchi maintain close cultural ties with Yupik peoples across the Bering Strait, recognizing ancient connections predating modern borders. Climate change now threatens both lifestyles‚Äîthinning ice endangers marine hunting while changing vegetation affects reindeer herding.
