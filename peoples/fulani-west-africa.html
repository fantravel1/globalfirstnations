Who Are the Fulani People?

The Fulani (also Fula, Fulbe, or Peul) are one of the world's largest nomadic pastoral groups, with approximately 40-50 million people spread across the Sahel and West Africa‚Äîfrom Senegal and Mauritania through Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger, Nigeria, Cameroon, Chad, and Central African Republic. They speak Fulfulde, a Niger-Congo language. The Fulani developed a distinctive cattle-centered culture; their migrations in search of pasture have taken them across the continent over centuries. They established powerful Islamic states in the 18th-19th centuries and remain economically and politically significant across the region.

Cattle Culture

Cattle define traditional Fulani identity. The Fulani developed the distinctive long-horned zebu cattle breeds suited to tropical conditions. Cattle provide milk, meat, hides, and wealth; bride prices are paid in cattle; social status correlates with herd size. Pastoral Fulani move seasonally between wet and dry season pastures, following rainfall and grass. The intimate knowledge of cattle health, breeding, and management constitutes specialized expertise. While many Fulani have settled in towns, cattle remain symbolically central. "Pulaaku"‚Äîthe Fulani code of behavior‚Äîincludes the virtue of caring properly for cattle.

Pulaaku: The Fulani Way

Pulaaku is the code of behavior defining Fulani identity‚Äîvalues including semteende (modesty, reserve), munyal (patience, self-control), hakkiilo (wisdom, foresight), and suudu (integrity). These values shape behavior: emotional restraint, dignified bearing, and self-sufficiency. Pulaaku distinguishes "true" Fulani from others and from assimilated Fulani who've abandoned pastoral life and values. The code emphasizes endurance of hardship without complaint‚Äîessential for pastoral life's difficulties. How strictly communities maintain pulaaku varies; urban Fulani may emphasize it differently than nomadic pastoralists.

Jihad States

In the 18th-19th centuries, Fulani Islamic scholars led jihads establishing theocratic states across West Africa. The Sokoto Caliphate (founded 1804 by Usman dan Fodio in present-day Nigeria) became one of Africa's largest states. Similar movements created the Imamate of Futa Jallon (Guinea), Futa Toro (Senegal), and Massina Empire (Mali). These jihads sought to purify Islam and establish sharia governance. Their legacies shape regional politics: traditional Fulani emirates retain influence; tensions between Fulani and other groups partly trace to these historical conquests.

Contemporary Conflicts

Fulani herders face increasing conflict with settled farmers across the Sahel. Climate change, population growth, and expanding agriculture have squeezed pastoral corridors; armed clashes over land and water kill thousands annually. In Nigeria's Middle Belt, Fulani-farmer conflicts have killed more than Boko Haram terrorism. The conflicts have ethnic and religious dimensions but are fundamentally about resource competition. States struggle to mediate; some Fulani face accusations of terrorism while viewing themselves as defending traditional livelihoods. Solutions require addressing land tenure, climate adaptation, and inter-group relations.
