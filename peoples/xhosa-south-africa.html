Who Are the Xhosa?

The Xhosa (amaXhosa) are South Africa's second-largest ethnic group, numbering approximately 8 million people concentrated in the Eastern Cape province. The Xhosa were among the first Bantu peoples to encounter European settlers, engaging in nine Frontier Wars against the British (1779-1879). Their language, isiXhosa, is famous for its click consonants borrowed from Khoisan languages. The Xhosa produced two of South Africa's most influential leaders: Nelson Mandela and Thabo Mbeki. Traditional culture emphasizes ancestor veneration, initiation rituals, and the role of the amaxhwele (diviners).

The Click Language

IsiXhosa is one of South Africa's 11 official languages and features three types of click consonants borrowed through centuries of contact with Khoisan peoples. These clicks (denoted as c, x, and q in writing) give Xhosa its distinctive sound. The language carries rich oral literature including izibongo (praise poetry), iintsomi (folktales), and izaci (proverbs). IsiXhosa and isiZulu are mutually intelligible to a significant degree, forming part of the Nguni language family. Miriam Makeba introduced Xhosa clicks to global audiences through her "Click Song."

Initiation and Identity

Ulwaluko, male initiation, remains central to Xhosa identity despite controversy over safety. Initiates spend weeks in the bush undergoing circumcision and intensive teaching about manhood responsibilities. Emerging abakhwetha (initiates) transition to ikrwala status (newly initiated men), marked by distinctive dress and behavioral expectations. The ritual establishes masculine identity and community belonging. Nontando Mgxaji and other Xhosa feminists have challenged the exclusive focus on male initiation, though female coming-of-age ceremonies (intonjane) also exist. Debates over traditional versus clinical circumcision continue.

The Cattle-Killing Movement

In 1856-57, a teenage prophet named Nongqawuse experienced visions instructing the Xhosa to kill their cattle and destroy their crops. Ancestors would then return, driving Europeans into the sea. Thousands followed her prophecy, resulting in massive starvation that killed perhaps 40,000 and broke Xhosa military power. Contemporary Xhosa Culture

Despite urbanization and modernization, Xhosa cultural practices persist. Beadwork and traditional dress appear at ceremonies. Diviners (amagqirha) and herbalists continue spiritual and healing practices. Ancestor rituals (imbeleko) welcome newborns; animal sacrifices mark important transitions. The Xhosa maintain strong connections to rural Eastern Cape homesteads even while working in cities. Artists, musicians, and writers draw on Xhosa traditions while engaging global influences. The Xhosa demonstrate how African cultures adapt and persist through centuries of colonial and modern challenge.
